## A typical slide sequence

The order below combines common practice with the WCN argument. Pages can be merged, but the logic should hold.

<Steps>
  <Step title="Cover and one sentence" description="Project name, stage label, and contact. State the positioning plainly; avoid slogans." />
  <Step title="Problem and opportunity" description="The structural failure in connecting capital and projects, supported by observable phenomena rather than adjectives." />
  <Step title="What WCN is" description="The network boundary and participants, and the difference from a platform or a single chain — one diagram over several paragraphs." />
  <Step title="Product and user path" description="The minimal business loop across nodes, Proof of Business, and the three ledgers, shown as a short flow." />
  <Step title="Architecture summary" description="A one-page view of the six-layer node pyramid and the three-ledger model. Detail points to the whitepaper." />
  <Step title="Proof of Business" description="Why business proof matters, how a result is verified, the main abuse scenarios, and the mitigations — including the parts not yet solved." />
  <Step title="AI agent layer" description="The scope of automation, the permission and logging boundaries, and the rule that an agent cannot sign, transfer, or commit without human endorsement." />
  <Step title="Economics and stages" description="The Carry waterfall and the staged assumptions, expressed as ranges or qualitatively at the current stage." />
  <Step title="Traction and milestones" description="Verifiable outputs aligned with roadmap stages. Future milestones use conditionals — if a threshold is reached, the next stage opens." />
  <Step title="Competition and position" description="A comparison with adjacent models. Draw boundaries; do not disparage a specific competitor." />
  <Step title="Team and advisors" description="Core members and relevant experience. Advisory roles and real involvement are stated truthfully." />
  <Step title="Ask and cooperation" description="The need for this round or partnership, the use of funds or resources, and the time window." />
  <Step title="Risk and legal summary" description="The key risks, and a clear note that the deck is not investment advice. Detail points to the Legal and Risk Statement and the whitepaper." />
</Steps>

## Division of labor with the whitepaper

<CardGrid cols={2}>
  <Card title="The deck carries" icon="mic">The narrative arc, the key points, and the hooks for live discussion. Diagrams and analogies help a non-expert audience build a mental model.</Card>
  <Card title="The whitepaper carries" icon="book-open">Definitions, assumptions, boundary conditions, and citable detail — for due diligence and legal review, step by step.</Card>
</CardGrid>

<Callout type="info">A good deck survives being forwarded without the speaker. Each slide title should stand as its own unit of information. Avoid pages that only make sense alongside a spoken script.</Callout>

---

## The quality line

<Statement>The deck must not soften a securities-sensitive statement to look cleaner, and it must not let an unaudited forecast stand in for a staged fact. An exaggerated claim breaks consistency with the whitepaper, the FAQ, and the Legal and Risk Statement, and it costs trust during due diligence.</Statement>

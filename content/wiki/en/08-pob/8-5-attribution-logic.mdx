## Basic principles

Attribution binds reward to the critical path, not to the attendance list. These principles hold across every closed-loop type.

<CardGrid>
  <Card title="Many participants is not many leads" icon="users">A close can involve lawyers, underwriters, introducers, and execution nodes. The lead binds to the irreplaceable critical path, not to everyone present.</Card>
  <Card title="Lead cannot be copied without limit" icon="lock">One Sealed event allows mutually exclusive leads or an explicit split ratio. Otherwise the record becomes a double-spent narrative.</Card>
  <Card title="The last step does not cover the chain" icon="scale">Attribution separates who set the deal structure, who introduced the decisive resource, and who closed the last mile. Evidence decides each, not sequence.</Card>
</CardGrid>

<Statement>Without attribution, PoB proves only the result. With attribution, PoB can support the harder question of who created distributable value.</Statement>

---

## How a closed loop is split

A total closed loop divides into named blocks of contribution, and each block binds to a node or an agent. The split must stay consistent with the evidence package and the timeline. A narrative that claims sole lead while conflicting with the same material is refused.

<CardGrid cols={2}>
  <Card title="Resource introduction" icon="handshake">Who brought the decisive project, capital, or channel into the loop, with a record of when and how.</Card>
  <Card title="Execution advancement" icon="route">Who moved the work from intent to a deliverable, leaving traceable advancing actions.</Card>
  <Card title="Risk removal" icon="shield">Who cleared the blocking condition that held the result, shown through a causal chain rather than presence.</Card>
  <Card title="Final-state sign-off" icon="circle-check">Who closed the outcome to its observable final state, matching the evidence and the dates.</Card>
</CardGrid>

A contribution split sits beside capital weight; the two combine but do not replace each other. Capital weight measures stake. Attribution measures contribution structure.

## Role semantics

Each role carries a fixed meaning at settlement. A contested split escalates to a Verdict, which binds the outcome.

<CardGrid cols={3}>
  <Card title="Lead node" icon="target">Carries primary responsibility for clearing the critical blocker or introducing the decisive term or resource; the material shows the causal chain to the final state.</Card>
  <Card title="Collaborating node" icon="handshake">Provides necessary but non-decisive support, or shares by an explicit contract ratio rather than counting as lead by default.</Card>
  <Card title="Beneficiary" icon="package">Value accrues to the host. This does not automatically equal the largest contribution.</Card>
  <Card title="Reviewer" icon="search">Verifies and leaves a trace. Does not take business-side attribution, unless governance directs otherwise.</Card>
  <Card title="Agent contribution" icon="bot">Enters the attribution discussion only when the output was officially adopted and shows in the evidence — a ticket, a sign-off, a merge.</Card>
</CardGrid>

## Reducing disputes in practice

Attribution disputes shrink when the framework is set early and the timeline is cross-aligned.

<CardGrid cols={2}>
  <Card title="Agree the framework early" icon="pen-line">Set the expected attribution at the Deal and task layer. PoB can still revise it, but early agreement lowers later dispute.</Card>
  <Card title="Cross-align the timeline" icon="clock">Correspondence, calendar, contract effective date, and onchain timestamps are aligned against each other.</Card>
  <Card title="Use the objection procedure" icon="message-square">A Sealed conclusion can be reviewed, but only with new material or a specific logical error — not an open-ended delay.</Card>
  <Card title="Bind to the critical path" icon="route">Reward follows whoever cleared the blocker, so heavy and hard-to-prove work is not lost to whoever appeared last.</Card>
</CardGrid>

<Callout type="tip">
Attribution quality decides whether nodes take on heavy, long-cycle, hard-to-prove loops. If the prize always goes to whoever shows up last, the network turns short-sighted — against the long-term logic of PoB.
</Callout>

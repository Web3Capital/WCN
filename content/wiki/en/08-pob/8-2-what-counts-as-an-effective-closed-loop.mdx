## The four conditions a valid closed loop must satisfy

A closed loop enters PoB only when all four conditions hold together. The difficulty is the real-world cost of proof and cross-verification, not a cryptographic puzzle.

<CardGrid cols={2}>
  <Card title="The result is externally observable" icon="eye">A signing, a transfer, a launch, a delivery, or a KPI met. The outcome shows in the world, not only in a private claim.</Card>
  <Card title="The evidence is verifiable" icon="search">Contracts, invoices, onchain records, partner sign-off, reviewable logs. Material an independent party can examine.</Card>
  <Card title="Responsibility is traceable" icon="route">Each advancing action maps to a specific node or agent, so the closed loop has named owners rather than a crowd.</Card>
  <Card title="The process is re-reviewable" icon="circle-check">A reviewer who re-examines the process reaches the same judgment. The conclusion is reproducible, not a one-time call.</Card>
</CardGrid>

<Statement>The result is externally observable, the evidence is verifiable, responsibility is traceable, and the process is re-reviewable — all four, or it does not count.</Statement>

## Closed-loop types and what valid evidence looks like

The conditions hold across every type of closed loop. The shape of the evidence package changes; the underlying standard does not. The examples below are illustrative; the exact list follows current network rules and review templates.

<CardGrid cols={2}>
  <Card title="Financing closed loop" icon="coins">Signed agreement, board resolution, bank deposit or escrow-release record, cap-table update under compliance. **Counterexample**: a term sheet with no signature and no payment.</Card>
  <Card title="Service closed loop" icon="wrench">Statement of work, milestone acceptance, deliverable hash or signed report, invoice aligned to delivery. **Counterexample**: an internal draft the client never confirmed.</Card>
  <Card title="Growth closed loop" icon="trending-up">Campaign contract, attribution export, reconciled conversion or revenue range, method described against a baseline. **Counterexample**: a screenshot with no timestamp that no third party can reconcile.</Card>
  <Card title="Liquidity closed loop" icon="droplet">Exchange or market-making agreement, onchain pool address and window-period volume, and required compliance disclosure. **Counterexample**: a verbal meeting confirmation alone.</Card>
  <Card title="Resource-access closed loop" icon="handshake">Institutional access letter, official record of an opened channel or data feed, named owner, and effective date. **Counterexample**: a swapped card or a single meeting with no follow-through.</Card>
  <Card title="Agent-execution closed loop" icon="bot">A clear record that a human or process adopted the agent output — a ticket closed, a merge, a customer approval. The adoption holds a causal chain to the result. **Counterexample**: a generated file that never entered a decision.</Card>
</CardGrid>

## The deal threshold, restated

A completed deal turns on signatures, fulfilled conditions, and settled funds — not on how many times the parties met. A valid closed loop works the same way. Only an event that clears the outcome threshold enters the value layer. However exciting the diligence, an unmet threshold does not enter the ledger.

<Callout type="tip">
First write down what counts. Then the exclusions in 8.3 have teeth. Otherwise the system drains under the pressure of "this should count," and the nodes serious about closing the loop are the ones harmed.
</Callout>

## Surface active ≠ efficient configuration

Judging from the news, social platforms, and live events, it seems like investments, partnerships, and market expansion are happening all the time in Web3. But from the real perspective of the project and investors:

<Callout type="warning">
"There are a lot of capital flows" does not mean "capital allocation is efficient." Many capital flows are noisy, short-cycle, and relationship-driven. Really high-quality, reusable and sustainable capital flows are still scarce.
</Callout>

A group of industry observations: Traditional VCs look at 200+ projects per person per year and invest in 3-5 projects; Crypto VCs may look at more than 3 times as many projects, but the quality of information is lower, the cost of due diligence is higher, and the deal break rate is higher. The core reason is not that there are not enough people, but that the system is not enough.

---

## Four types of core friction

### 1. Screening costs are too high

"Seeing many items" does not mean "seeing the right item". Project information is inconsistent, quality varies greatly, background is opaque, and progress is difficult to verify, resulting in investors having to spend a lot of time on preliminary screening.

<CardGrid cols={2}>
  <Card title="Projects are difficult to judge quickly" icon="circle-help">The information is inconsistent, the materials are incomplete, and the narrative is unclear, making it difficult for investors to make quick judgments.</Card>
  <Card title="Capital preferences are difficult to map" icon="target">Stage preferences, track preferences, and ticket preferences are scattered in the human brain and are not structured.</Card>
  <Card title="A large number of projects are not prepared" icon="clipboard-list">Lack of clear financing structure, legal preparation and evidence of business progress.</Card>
  <Card title="High-value opportunities are drowned out by the noise" icon="radio">Really good projects and really suitable capital find it difficult to meet each other because of the noise.</Card>
</CardGrid>

### 2. The cost of trust is too high

Many projects and capitals do not lack a one-time opportunity to get to know each other, but what they lack is a sufficient foundation of trust and verification. Without a credible middle layer, review layer, and promotion layer, capital will continue to be conservative, and projects will continue to feel "difficult to obtain funds."

<CardGrid cols={2}>
  <Card title="Is the team credible?" icon="user">Whether background, experience, and track record can stand verification.</Card>
  <Card title="Is the structure clear?" icon="ruler">Is there a clear framework for legal affairs, rights, obligations and risks?</Card>
  <Card title="Is the progress real?" icon="chart-bar">Are milestones, deliverables and commitments documented.</Card>
  <Card title="Is the docking party reliable?" icon="handshake">Whether the introducer or intermediary is willing to take responsibility for the authenticity of the information.</Card>
</CardGrid>

### 3. Promotion costs are too high

Even when a project reaches potential investors, the process often breaks down in the middle.

<CardGrid cols={2}>
  <Card title="No one moved forward after the meeting" icon="pause">I've seen it, talked about it, and I feel good about it, but I lack the owner to follow up.</Card>
  <Card title="Insufficient materials" icon="paperclip">The project party does not know what the investors really lack, and the investors are not willing to repeat education.</Card>
  <Card title="There is no unified Deal space" icon="folder-open">Minutes, materials, status, and next steps are scattered in different channels.</Card>
  <Card title="Responsibility cannot be locked" icon="lock-open">Who will continue to follow, who will urge, who will organize, and who will be responsible for the results - it is often not clear.</Card>
</CardGrid>

### 4. The results are difficult to precipitate

Even if financing or strategic cooperation is achieved, the result is still a one-time transaction.

<CardGrid cols={2}>
  <Card title="No unified evidence filing" icon="package">Cooperation was achieved, but the evidence was not systematically deposited.</Card>
  <Card title="No unified contribution attribution" icon="tag">Many participants helped, but no one can say who actually drove the results.</Card>
  <Card title="There is no unified settlement entrance" icon="credit-card">The results are difficult to account for in long-term credit, PoB, or network value systems.</Card>
  <Card title="Unable to form a reuse flywheel" icon="refresh-cw">One success is not precipitated into a more efficient standard process next time.</Card>
</CardGrid>

## Inefficient capital flows drive up system costs across the industry

<CardGrid cols={2}>
  <Card title="Project parties are forced to spend a lot of time financing" icon="clock">The founding team repeatedly changed the deck, found people, explained, supplemented information, made appointments, and followed up. Without system assistance, these actions continue to consume precious execution time.</Card>
  <Card title="Capitalists are forced to do repeated screening" icon="search">Investors should spend their time on high-value judgment and allocation, but in reality a lot of time is consumed in preliminary screening, confirmation and low-trust communication.</Card>
  <Card title="Servers and collaborators cannot access stably" icon="plug">When the financing process and collaboration process are unstable, it is also difficult for roles such as legal, security, research, brand, and growth to enter the workflow earlier.</Card>
  <Card title="The entire industry relies more on favors than systems" icon="drama">When there is no unified routing layer, capital allocation relies more on "who knows whom" rather than a more transparent, efficient, and replicable network structure.</Card>
</CardGrid>

## Entry point for WCN

One of the core values ​​of WCN is to upgrade "capital contact at the relationship level" to "capital flow at the system level."

<Statement>
It is not about exposing projects to more investors, but about allowing more suitable capital to enter a high-quality closed loop faster through a clearer structure, lower friction and stronger execution system.
</Statement>

## Core conclusion

Inefficient capital flows are not a single problem, but are caused by four structural frictions:

<CardGrid cols={2}>
  <Card title="Screening costs are too high">Information is unstructured, and investors and project parties are looking for needles in a haystack of noise.</Card>
  <Card title="The cost of trust is too high">There is a lack of credible middle and review layers, capital is conservative, and project parties are tired.</Card>
  <Card title="Promotion costs are too high">Deal breaks frequently in the middle stage, lacking owner, space, and closed loop.</Card>
  <Card title="Sunk costs are too high">As a result, it cannot be turned into a network asset, and every cooperation starts from scratch.</Card>
</CardGrid>

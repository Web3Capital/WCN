## Contribution is not the problem. Provable contribution is.

When a project secures funding, one role rarely drives it alone. When a partnership lands, one node rarely closes it. Referral, research, documentation, growth execution, relationship advancement, risk management, and service delivery all shape the outcome.

<Callout type="warning">
Most of that influence stays at "everyone knows someone did something." It never reaches "the system can prove who moved the result."
</Callout>

Consider a closed seed round. An introducer opened the door. An investor ran diligence and led the round. A service provider built the legal structure. A market maker later committed liquidity. How should the value split across the four? Today the answer is the same everywhere: there is no standard answer. It falls to relationships, negotiation, and whoever speaks loudest.

## Four root causes

### 1. Most contribution happens before the result

Before a business loop closes, much of the decisive work is already done: the introduction, the screening, the organizing, the materials, the conversations, the resource coordination. Each one shapes the outcome. Because none is the final signature, the system tends to overlook them.

<Callout type="info">
Traditional finance locks roles and splits before a deal begins, through a mandate letter, an engagement letter, and a fee schedule. Web3 has almost no such front-end structure. Contribution happens in a gray area, and the split is settled after the fact.
</Callout>

### 2. Contribution leaves no structured evidence

Much critical collaboration still happens in private chats, calls, offline meetings, and one-off documents. As a result, even work that clearly occurred cannot be reconstructed afterward.

<CardGrid cols={2}>
  <Card title="No shared event ID" icon="hash">Each collaboration has no unique identifier, so it cannot be tracked.</Card>
  <Card title="No shared evidence package" icon="package">Contracts, notes, reports, and emails stay scattered and incomplete.</Card>
  <Card title="No shared role declaration" icon="drama">Who took part, and in what role, is never recorded in one structure.</Card>
  <Card title="No shared state machine" icon="chart-bar">What stage a deal has reached is not tracked to a standard.</Card>
</CardGrid>

### 3. Volume stands in for contribution

Without a clear record and a review mechanism, attribution drifts toward whoever is most visible rather than whoever did the work.

<CardGrid cols={2}>
  <Card title="Whoever is loudest" icon="megaphone">The most vocal participant is the most likely to be seen.</Card>
  <Card title="Whoever sits nearest the center" icon="target">A prominent position gets credit by default.</Card>
  <Card title="Whoever signs last" icon="flag">The final signature can overwrite the value of everyone who came before.</Card>
  <Card title="Whoever tells the best story" icon="file-text">Narrative ability substitutes for evidence.</Card>
</CardGrid>

### 4. No unified attribution rules

In most collaborations, no shared rule decides the questions that settlement depends on.

<CardGrid cols={2}>
  <Card title="Who led" icon="crown">The first to introduce, or the one who carried it until the result held?</Card>
  <Card title="Who collaborated" icon="handshake">Which roles gave decisive support without leading?</Card>
  <Card title="Does an Agent count" icon="bot">When an Agent does critical work, how is its value recorded?</Card>
  <Card title="What does not count" icon="ban">How are verbal help, repeated claims, and fruitless actions excluded?</Card>
</CardGrid>

## When settlement fails, incentives fail with it

<CardGrid cols={2}>
  <Card title="The valuable people leave" icon="door-open">If real contribution cannot be proven over time, the people who actually close loops are the first to lose faith, and they move to systems that pay back.</Card>
  <Card title="Noise outpays results" icon="megaphone">When the system cannot prove results, it rewards high-frequency posting, audience-building, and exposure over closing and delivery.</Card>
  <Card title="Collaboration turns short-term" icon="timer">Without a clear settlement expectation, people take only the visible near-term action and avoid long, complex work.</Card>
  <Card title="The network builds no credit" icon="trending-down">If every result stays at "it got done once" and never enters a structured ledger, no long-term reputation can form.</Card>
</CardGrid>

<Callout type="warning">
No proof of contribution, no stable incentive. No stable incentive, no high-quality network.
</Callout>

## Why proof of contribution sits at the center of WCN

WCN does not start by handing out rewards. It starts by defining what counts as a result, what counts as evidence, and what counts as an effective contribution.

<Statement>
The problem WCN solves is not how to offer more incentives. It is how to turn the work that genuinely creates value into objects that can be verified, attributed, and settled.
</Statement>

## What this chapter establishes

Contribution is hard to settle not because contribution is missing, but because four things are.

<CardGrid cols={2}>
  <Card title="No shared event structure" icon="building-2">No unique ID, role declaration, or stage tracking for each collaboration.</Card>
  <Card title="No credible evidence chain" icon="link">Contracts, notes, receipts, and onchain records are not filed in one place.</Card>
  <Card title="No clear attribution rules" icon="scale">No standard definition of lead, collaborator, support, or Agent contribution.</Card>
  <Card title="No settlement entry" icon="coins">So contribution never reaches long-term credit, PoB, or the value layer.</Card>
</CardGrid>

<Callout type="tip">
This is why Proof of Business (PoB) is not an extra module. It is the institutional foundation the rest of the network stands on.
</Callout>

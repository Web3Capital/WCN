## The expensive lesson: chain first, business later

Over the past five years, "build the chain, then find the use case" has been the most common path in Web3 — and one of the most likely to fail.

<CardGrid cols={2}>
  <Card title="EOS" icon="ban">In 2018 it raised 4.1 billion USD, the largest ICO on record, and built a high-performance L1. It lacked a defining application, and the ecosystem never formed. The rebrand to Antelope in 2023 left business density near zero. The problem was not the technology. It was the absence of business.</Card>
  <Card title="Internet Computer (ICP)" icon="ban">Launched in 2021, the Dfinity Foundation raised 166 million USD. The technology was strong — full web applications onchain — but real use stayed thin, and the token fell from 700 USD to 5 USD. "What can it do" and "will anyone use it" are two different questions.</Card>
  <Card title="Aptos and Sui" icon="triangle-alert">Launched in 2022 and 2023, each raised more than 350 million USD. The technical metrics on throughput and latency led the field, but early TVL leaned on liquidity incentives and fell sharply once they ended. Infrastructure came first; business density did not.</Card>
  <Card title="The L2 wave" icon="ban">More than forty new L2s launched across 2023 and 2024 — Blast, Mode, Manta, Mantle, and others. Most drew TVL through airdrops, and real users and volume stayed low once incentives stopped. Build the bridge, then look for cargo: the model failed repeatedly.</Card>
</CardGrid>

<Callout type="warning">
These projects share one trait, and it is not weak technology. They took on onchain complexity without business density. The costs of identity, assets, governance, oversight, and user experience all arrived first, with too few real transactions to carry them.
</Callout>

## The path that works: business first, then chain

The most successful onchain projects in Web3 nearly all follow the reverse order — build the business, then build the chain.

<CardGrid cols={2}>
  <Card title="Binance → BNB Chain" icon="circle-check">Binance launched a centralized exchange in 2017 and accumulated tens of millions of users and deep transaction data. BNB Chain followed in 2020 and quickly became a center of DeFi, carried by that traffic. The value of the chain rested on the business.</Card>
  <Card title="Coinbase → Base" icon="circle-check">Coinbase spent a decade building a large compliant exchange with around 110 million verified users. Base L2 launched in 2023 and carried real volume from day one, without airdrop stimulus. Base succeeded on Coinbase's business density, not on the OP Stack alone.</Card>
  <Card title="Stripe → crypto payments" icon="circle-check">Stripe spent fourteen years building large-scale online payments and processing trillions of dollars. It acquired Bridge, a stablecoin payments firm, in 2024, on the merchant network and compliance system already in place. Business density set the pace of the crypto layer.</Card>
  <Card title="Circle and USDC" icon="circle-check">Circle did not build a chain first. It built stablecoin issuance and a compliance framework. USDC circulates across many chains, and Circle's value binds to its business relationships — banks, exchanges, merchants — not to any single chain.</Card>
</CardGrid>

> The rule: for projects that succeed onchain, the chain is not the starting point. It is a natural extension once business density passes a critical point. Secure the cargo, then build the road — not the reverse.

## Why WCN must build the business first

If WCN forced full onchain deployment today, it would meet four problems.

<CardGrid cols={2}>
  <Card title="Node identity onchain" icon="link">Nodes hold too little collaboration record to prove the value of an identity. The onchain NFT becomes a collectible, not a work credential.</Card>
  <Card title="PoB records onchain" icon="mail">The volume and quality of loops cannot yet support a credible value ledger. The onchain data reads as noise, not signal.</Card>
  <Card title="Settlement onchain" icon="scale">The objects and rules of settlement still iterate. Hardening them into smart contracts too early constrains the business model.</Card>
  <Card title="Governance onchain" icon="landmark">Nodes and community are not yet mature enough to bear the coordination cost of decentralized governance. Onchain voting becomes a game for the few.</Card>
</CardGrid>

<Callout type="info">
The core tension: the benefits of going onchain — tamper resistance, transparency, trustlessness — only land once business density is sufficient. Below that, going onchain only adds complexity, slows iteration, and raises the cold-start threshold.
</Callout>

## The phased path

<CardGrid cols={1}>
  <Card title="Phase 1: Web2 tools and the node network" icon="globe">WCN uses mature Web2 infrastructure — a Next.js application, a database, APIs — to recruit nodes, run deals and tasks, and start agent execution. What it verifies is the collaboration model, not the onchain architecture.</Card>
  <Card title="Phase 2: the PoB ledger gathers density" icon="clipboard-list">Loops keep closing, and the rules of evidence and attribution stabilize in practice. Once the PoB ledger holds sufficient density and credibility, it is worth permanent record.</Card>
  <Card title="Phase 3: selective onchain bearing" icon="link-2">WCN migrates the data that most needs tamper resistance — PoB summaries, node reputation snapshots, settlement records — onchain. It chooses the chain that fits on cost, speed, and compliance, rather than binding to one.</Card>
  <Card title="Phase 4: the asset and governance layers" icon="building-2">Once the network matures, the PoB ledger is credible, and settlement rules are stable, WCN considers onchain asset issuance and decentralized governance. By then, going onchain follows naturally rather than being forced.</Card>
</CardGrid>

## Common questions

<CardGrid cols={2}>
  <Card title="Is this just Web2?" icon="circle-help">No. The endgame includes onchain identity, onchain PoB, onchain settlement, and a possible token. Those layers gain meaning only once the business network matures. Web2 tools are a means, not the end.</Card>
  <Card title="How is it trustworthy without a chain?" icon="circle-help">In the near term, trust comes from the business outcome itself — whether the deal closed, the project was funded, the service was delivered. Going onchain is a long-term trust layer, not the only source of trust today.</Card>
  <Card title="Will competitors go onchain first and take the market?" icon="circle-help">History shows that an onchain project without business density forms no moat, even with a head start. The real advantages — node density, PoB data, agent training quality — take time and cannot be bought by going onchain.</Card>
  <Card title="Will investors find it too little Web3?" icon="circle-help">A careful investor reads whether the business logic holds, not the technical label. Binance was a purely centralized product early on, and its business density gave BNB Chain a real foundation. WCN follows the same logic.</Card>
</CardGrid>

<Callout type="tip">
WCN's endgame can hold a chain, a token, and full onchain governance. Its starting point must be the business network. The chain is the result, not the premise — the path proven by Binance, Coinbase, and Stripe.
</Callout>

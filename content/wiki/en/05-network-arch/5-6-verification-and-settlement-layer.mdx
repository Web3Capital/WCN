## The question this layer answers: how does a result become trustworthy?

However capable the first four layers are, without L5 the system cannot separate done from claimed to be done. This layer upgrades a subjective narrative into an auditable chain of evidence.

<Statement>The work of L5: turning "the result happened" into "the result was verified, attributed, and settled."</Statement>

---

## The three ledgers and the Verification Node

WCN keeps three parallel ledgers — the Project ledger, the Capital ledger, and the Proof ledger. Reconciliation runs along the midline that joins them. When a business outcome reconciles across the three, it produces a Verification Node: an anchor that can be checked independently.

<CardGrid cols={3}>
  <Card title="Project ledger" icon="package">What was raised, scoped, and executed — the record of the work itself.</Card>
  <Card title="Capital ledger" icon="coins">What was committed, deployed, and settled — the record of the money.</Card>
  <Card title="Proof ledger" icon="circle-check">The verifiable, tamper-evident record of outcomes, joined to the other two by reconciliation.</Card>
</CardGrid>

---

## How verification and settlement run

A result does not enter the record on a claim. It enters once evidence reconciles and a reviewer confirms it.

<Steps>
  <Step title="Evidence submission">The deal owner submits the result evidence — signed agreement, payment record, deliverable, onchain hash, minutes, confirmation. What is required depends on the deal: a financing deal needs the agreement and payment confirmation; a service deal needs the scope and acceptance.</Step>
  <Step title="Independent review">A reviewer checks the evidence for completeness, authenticity, a plausible timeline, and risk. A reviewer cannot be a participant in the deal, and the result is confirmed by multiple signatures.</Step>
  <Step title="Verification Node">On passing review, reconciliation produces a Verification Node on the Proof ledger. The record is immutable and carries the participants, an evidence summary, the attribution, and timestamps.</Step>
  <Step title="Settlement">Verified records are aggregated and settled on a defined schedule. Value is allocated to contributors — nodes, service providers, and agents — by their attribution.</Step>
</Steps>

## What makes a business loop valid

Proof of Business records a verifiable business outcome, not participation. A loop counts only when four conditions hold.

<CardGrid cols={2}>
  <Card title="Externally observable" icon="eye">The result is visible outside the system — a signing, a transfer, a launch, a delivery, a met target.</Card>
  <Card title="Verifiable evidence" icon="paperclip">Contracts, receipts, onchain records, partner confirmation, and reviewable logs support the result.</Card>
  <Card title="Traceable responsibility" icon="search">Each step that advanced the outcome maps to a specific node or agent.</Card>
  <Card title="Reviewable process" icon="repeat">A reviewer, re-examining the process, reaches the same conclusion.</Card>
</CardGrid>

## The states of a record

On the Proof ledger, a record moves through defined states. Each is a precise term, not a casual one.

<CardGrid cols={3}>
  <Card title="Verified" icon="circle-check">The record has passed reconciliation across the three ledgers.</Card>
  <Card title="Settled" icon="scale">A transaction on the Capital ledger has completed its settlement.</Card>
  <Card title="Sealed" icon="lock">The record is locked — a final state that can no longer change.</Card>
</CardGrid>

<Callout type="info">
A dispute or audit resolves in a Verdict: a conclusion that cannot be appealed. Proof of Business is an internal mechanism, but its evidence chain can be retrieved and re-examined by external auditors and regulators — evidence-backed, not self-asserted.
</Callout>

---

## Real-time, not year-end

Traditional verification is an audit after the fact — reviewed once the period closes. WCN embeds verification in the moment.

<CardGrid cols={2}>
  <Card title="Traditional: post-action audit" icon="landmark">Established and trustworthy, but centralized, gated, and reviewed after the period — which leaves a gap between the action and its confirmation.</Card>
  <Card title="WCN: real-time embedding" icon="globe">Each deal enters evidence submission and review as it completes. The evidence chain stays open to external audit, so there is no year-end catch-up.</Card>
</CardGrid>

---

## What fails without this layer

<CardGrid cols={2}>
  <Card title="Results stay subjective" icon="ban">A participant claims to have driven a deal, with no evidence behind the claim. The system falls back to words and relationships.</Card>
  <Card title="Attribution is contested" icon="ban">Several parties join one deal, and no one can establish who contributed what. Most projects avoid the question, which discourages real contribution over time.</Card>
  <Card title="Reputation does not build" icon="ban">Without verified results, a node's reputation cannot form. The system cannot tell a high-value node from a low-value one.</Card>
  <Card title="Value does not accumulate" icon="ban">A collaboration closes and dissolves, leaving no record. The network cannot learn from past work or compound on it.</Card>
</CardGrid>

<Callout type="tip">
The verification and settlement layer is not a patch on the final step. It is the reason the architecture exists. It gives every collaboration a trustworthy endpoint, every contribution a measurable value, and turns the network from one-off cooperation into a system that accumulates.
</Callout>

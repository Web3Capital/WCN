## The question this layer answers: who receives the demand?

A project enters L1 with a need. L2 matches it to the right capital. Fit is more than having money to deploy.

<CardGrid cols={2}>
  <Card title="Track fit" icon="target">Whether the capital's preferred sectors — DeFi, AI, RWA, infrastructure, gaming — match the project.</Card>
  <Card title="Stage fit" icon="chart-bar">Seed, pre-A, A, strategic, or OTC. Each stage carries different evaluation criteria and a different decision cycle.</Card>
  <Card title="Structure fit" icon="ruler">SAFE, SAFT, token warrant, equity, or convertible note. Whether the instrument preference is compatible.</Card>
  <Card title="Region and compliance fit" icon="globe">Whether the capital's KYC and AML requirements, jurisdictional limits, and regional focus align with the project.</Card>
</CardGrid>

## Relationship-based versus mandate-based allocation

<CardGrid cols={2}>
  <Card title="Traditional: mandate-based" icon="landmark">A manager works to a clear mandate set by its agreement with backers — constraints on sector, region, stage, and ticket. Allocation runs as a system, recorded and repeatable rather than ad hoc.</Card>
  <Card title="Web3: relationship-based" icon="smartphone">Most crypto investment decisions still rest on a partner's relationships and judgment. Preferences are not registered, matching is not systematic, and the path from project to capital stays unpredictable.</Card>
</CardGrid>

<Callout type="info">
L2 does not replace investor judgment, which stays a human capability. It systematizes the steps before the judgment — so a fitting project reaches fitting capital faster, with less reliance on finding someone to make the introduction.
</Callout>

---

## What enters the system

<CardGrid cols={2}>
  <Card title="Crypto-native funds" icon="coins">Funds focused on the primary market. They favor early, technically driven projects with a token-economic design. Decisions tend to be fast, and the threshold to enter is high.</Card>
  <Card title="Family offices and private wealth" icon="landmark">Longer-horizon, more patient capital. It favors projects backed by real revenue or assets. Decisions tend to be slower, and relationships run deep.</Card>
  <Card title="Strategic capital" icon="handshake">The strategic arms of exchanges, chains, and protocols. They weigh ecosystem synergy alongside return, and can bring traffic, technical collaboration, and listing access.</Card>
  <Card title="Regional capital connectors" icon="globe">Nodes that link a region's capital pools to global projects. Local knowledge is the core capability they contribute.</Card>
</CardGrid>

## How allocation works in WCN

<Steps>
  <Step title="Preference registration">Capital registers its preferences on entry: track, stage, ticket range, region, instruments, and exclusions. The preferences form a capital profile.</Step>
  <Step title="Matching">When a project enters L1, the system recommends fitting capital from the registered profiles. A research agent attaches a project summary and the basis for the match.</Step>
  <Step title="Structured deal access">Capital reviews the structured information and decides whether to enter the deal room. Entry commits time and attention to a deeper assessment.</Step>
  <Step title="Advancement and record">Capital advances diligence, raises questions, and negotiates terms in the deal room. Every interaction is logged, which feeds later attribution and reputation.</Step>
</Steps>

<Callout type="tip">
The capital allocation layer is the hinge between L1 and L3. Without it, a project is only demand. With it, demand can enter a deal process and a structured allocation. The goal is not to replace judgment, but to raise the efficiency of judgment.
</Callout>
